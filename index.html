Bio

My research focuses on the carbon cycle in terrestrial ecosystems. Under climate change, it is uncertain whether the terrestrial biosphere will continue serving as a carbon sink. To answer this question requires a robust understanding of how photosynthesis responds to climate. My research aims to understand patterns and variability in terrestrial photosynthesis through the synergy between observations and process-based models. I have a particular interest in novel tracers for photosynthesis observations — carbonyl sulfide and solar induced chlorophyll fluorescence. At Carnegie Global Ecology, I work with Dr. Michalak to use geostatistical approaches to constrain the patterns of photosynthesis in North America.

I received my Ph.D. in Atmospheric and Oceanic Sciences from UCLA and B.Sc. in Geology from Nanjing University. My dissertation work used chamber observations and reactive transport modeling to understand carbonyl sulfide exchange between soils and the atmosphere. My latest work explored applications of carbonyl sulfide as a leaf-scale tracer for stomatal conductance.

Besides my main research activities, I am interested in scientific programming and data science. I use Python, Julia, and other high-level programming languages to solve my research problems, and occasionally contribute to my software packages.

Research interests

  • Biosphere–atmosphere exchange of carbon, water, trace gases, and energy
  • Photosynthesis, chlorophyll fluorescence, and stomatal conductance
  • Multiphase reactive transport of trace gases in soils
  • Applications of statistical learning in biogeosciences
